Chapter 1: Postulates of Quantum Mechanics

We begin with the Postulates of Quantum Mechanics in an attempt to set the framework for which quantum computing is performed. If and when you get confused about “why,” it is good practice to remember the definition of a postulate: a thing suggested or assumed to be true as the basis for reasoning, discussion, or belief.

Postulate 1 of Quantum Mechanics

An isolated system is associated with a complex vector space with an inner product known as the state space. The system is described by its state vector, which is a unit vector in the system’s state space.

Inner products (dot product → quantum inner product)

The dot product of two real vectors is:

$$ \vec{v} \cdot \vec{w} = |\vec{v}|\,|\vec{w}|\cos\theta $$

In component form:

$$ \vec{v} \cdot \vec{w} = v_x w_x + v_y w_y. $$

To compute this using matrices, we use the transpose:

$$ \vec{v}^T \vec{w} = \begin{bmatrix} v_x & v_y \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} w_x \\ w_y \end{bmatrix}. $$

The length of a vector is:

$$ \|\vec{v}\| = \sqrt{\vec{v} \cdot \vec{v}}. $$

For quantum states, we generalize the dot product into the inner product:

$$ \langle \phi | \psi \rangle = \phi_1^* \psi_1 + \phi_2^* \psi_2. $$

Here, the bra vector is the conjugate-transpose of the ket:

$$ \langle \phi | = (|\phi\rangle)^\dagger. $$

This ensures inner products are real and positive when taking \( \langle \psi | \psi \rangle \).

The Qubit

A qubit is the simplest nontrivial quantum system. It has two basis states:

$$ |0\rangle = \begin{bmatrix}1 \\ 0\end{bmatrix}, \qquad |1\rangle = \begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 1\end{bmatrix}. $$

A general qubit is written:

$$ |\psi\rangle = \alpha |0\rangle + \beta |1\rangle, $$ with normalization: $$ |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1. $$

A linear combination of basis states is known as a superposition.

Compute:

\( \langle 0 | 0 \rangle \), \( \langle 1 | 1 \rangle \), \( \langle 0 | 1 \rangle \), \( \langle 1 | 0 \rangle \).

Superposition Example

Example:

$$ |\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} |0\rangle + \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}} |1\rangle. $$

This is a valid quantum state because:

$$ \left|\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\right|^2 + \left|\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\right|^2 = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{2}{3} = 1. $$

Postulate 2 of Quantum Mechanics

The evolution of a closed quantum system is described by a unitary transformation.

$$ |\psi_f\rangle = U |\psi_i\rangle $$

A unitary operator satisfies:

$$ U^\dagger U = I. $$

Unitarity preserves the norm of the state vector, preserving probabilities as required by Postulate 1.

Pauli Matrices

These fundamental quantum gates are written:

$$ X = \begin{bmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0\end{bmatrix}, \quad Y = \begin{bmatrix}0 & -i \\ i & 0\end{bmatrix}, \quad Z = \begin{bmatrix}1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1\end{bmatrix}. $$

Applying them to a general qubit:

  • X-gate: bit flip
  • Y-gate: bit + phase flip
  • Z-gate: phase flip only
Show that \( X^\dagger X = Y^\dagger Y = Z^\dagger Z = I \).

Bloch Sphere Parameterization

We write amplitudes in polar form:

$$ \alpha = \cos(\theta/2), \qquad \beta = e^{i\phi} \sin(\theta/2). $$

This gives the Bloch sphere representation:

$$ |\psi\rangle = \cos\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|0\rangle + e^{i\phi} \sin\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right)|1\rangle. $$

The parameters \( \theta \) and \( \phi \) map naturally to spherical coordinates, where:

  • \( \theta = 0 \Rightarrow |0\rangle \)
  • \( \theta = \pi \Rightarrow |1\rangle \)
  • \( \phi \) is the azimuthal rotation around the z-axis

$$ \theta' = \pi - \theta, \qquad \phi' = \phi + \pi. $$

Thus, the X-gate rotates the qubit 180° about the x-axis.

Hadamard Gate

The Hadamard operator is:

$$ H = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}. $$

Acting on \( |0\rangle \):

$$ H|0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle). $$

This creates superposition.

Verify that \( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle) \) is normalized.

Postulate 2′ — Schrödinger Equation

The time evolution of a closed quantum system is given by:

$$ i\hbar \frac{d}{dt} |\psi(t)\rangle = H |\psi(t)\rangle. $$

The Hamiltonian \(H\) is Hermitian:

$$ H^\dagger = H. $$

Deriving exponential time evolution

The only operators satisfying continuity, composition, and unitarity are:

$$ U(t) = e^{-iHt/\hbar}. $$

Taking the derivative:

$$ \frac{d}{dt} e^{-iHt/\hbar} = -\frac{i}{\hbar} H e^{-iHt/\hbar}, $$ recovering the Schrödinger equation.

Energy eigenvalue equation

If:

$$ H|E\rangle = E|E\rangle, $$

then the time-dependent solution is:

$$ |\psi(t)\rangle = e^{-iEt/\hbar}|E\rangle. $$

Eigenvalues of \(H\) give the allowed energies, and eigenvectors are the stationary states.

If \( H = \hbar\omega X \), compute its eigenvalues and eigenvectors.
