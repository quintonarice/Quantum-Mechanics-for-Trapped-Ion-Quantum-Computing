Postulates of Quantum Mechanics

We begin with the Postulates of Quantum Mechanics in an attempt to set the framework for which quantum computing is performed. If and when you get confused about “why” its good practice to remember the definition of a postulate: a thing suggested or assumed as true as the basis for reasoning, discussion, or belief.

Postulate 1 of Quantum Mechanics

An isolated system is associated with a complex vector space with an inner product known as the state space. The system is described by its state vector which is a unit vector in the system’s state space.

In real space (2D), we describe a vector using its components:

\[ \vec{v} = v_x \hat{x} + v_y \hat{y} \tag{1.1} \]

Where \\v_x\\ and \\v_y\\ are the x- and y-components while and are unit vectors (vectors with magnitude 1). This vector can also be represented as a column matrix:

\[ \vec{v} = \begin{pmatrix} v_x \\ v_y \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.2} \]

Similarly, a complex vector space can be expressed as a column matrix, however, the components are allowed to be complex by numbers.

\[ \psi = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{pmatrix}, \quad \alpha,\beta \in \mathbb{C} \tag{1.3} \]

The Greek letter psi, $\psi$ is famously used for quantum state vectors so we shall also adopt it here. It is encased in what is known as a ket, |ψ⟩, in Dirac notation and therefore the quantum state vector is commonly pronounced “ket psi.” An important feature of real vectors is the dot product, which gives the geometric relation between two vectors and allows for calculation of the length of a vector. The dot product of two vectors is given by:

\[ \vec{v}\cdot\vec{w} = |\vec{v}||\vec{w}|\cos\theta \tag{1.4} \]

Where the absolute values indicate the magnitude or length and θ is the angle between the two vectors. The dot product can also be expressed in component form as:

\[ \vec{v}\cdot\vec{w} = v_x w_x + v_y w_y \tag{1.5} \]

Where the unit vectors vanish using the first definition of the dot product given. Notice the dot product is a scalar quantity. We can compute the same result using the matrix multiplication with a small caveat. To compute the matrix dot product, we must transpose the first vector so that the number of columns in the 1st matrix equals the number of rows in the 2nd matrix. So that:

\[ \vec{v}\cdot\vec{w} = \begin{pmatrix} v_x & v_y \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} w_x \\ w_y \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.6} \]

Another important feature is that the dot product of a vector with itself gives the length of the vector:

\[ \vec{v}\cdot\vec{v} = v_x^2 + v_y^2 \tag{1.7} \]

Or

\[ |\vec{v}| = \sqrt{\vec{v}\cdot\vec{v}} \tag{1.8} \]

Understanding that the dot product for real vectors is computed using the transpose in addition to the length of the vector being the square root of its dot product with itself is valuable because the quantum state vector has the similar albeit more general properties. In fact, the “quantum dot product” is the generalized dot product known as the inner product. For example, the inner product of two quantum state vectors, and :

\[ \langle \phi | \psi \rangle \tag{1.9} \]

Where ⟨ϕ|, called “bra phi,” is the conjugate-transpose of ket phi, |ϕ⟩. The inner product is therefore also called a bra-ket.

Now, for the bra-ket via matrix multiplication:

\[ \langle \phi | \psi \rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \phi_1^* & \phi_2^* \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \psi_1 \\ \psi_2 \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.10} \]

So that:

\[ \langle \psi | \psi \rangle = |\psi_1|^2 + |\psi_2|^2 \tag{1.11} \]

Therefore,

\[ |\psi| = \sqrt{\langle \psi | \psi \rangle} \tag{1.12} \]

By postulate 1, |ψ⟩ must be a unit vector, hence:

\[ \langle \psi | \psi \rangle = 1 \tag{1.13} \]

The conjugate-transpose of the inner product is what keeps postulate 1 intact. That is why we use it rather than just the regular transpose.

Postulate 1 implies that the state of a quantum mechanical system, its quantum state (which includes all knowable information about the system), is represented by a normalized state vector known as ket psi: |ψ⟩.

Example: The Qubit

The simplest non-trivial quantum mechanical system is the qubit. A qubit has a two-dimensional state space namely, |0⟩ and |1⟩ which form an orthonormal basis. “Ortho” meaning orthogonal to each other and normal meaning normalized to 1. You show orthonormality using the inner product.

Use the inner product to show orthonormality of |0⟩ and |1⟩.

The state vector of a qubit is commonly written as:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle \tag{1.14} \]

The qubit states |0⟩ and |1⟩ can be expressed as column vectors:

\[ |0\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad |1\rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.15} \]

From Postulate 1, normalization requires that the qubit state vector’s inner product with itself is equal to 1.

\[ \langle \psi | \psi \rangle = 1 \tag{1.16} \]

Which implies that:

\[ |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1 \tag{1.17} \]

Again, the qubit is state is a linear combination. In general, any linear combination is a superposition of the states:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \sum_i c_i |\psi_i\rangle \tag{1.18} \]

Example:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle) \tag{1.19} \]

This state is a superposition with amplitudes of and , respectively and is a valid quantum state according to postulate 1.

Postulate 2 of Quantum Mechanics

The evolution of a closed quantum system is described by a unitary transformation.

\[ |\psi'\rangle = U|\psi\rangle \tag{1.20} \]

Where |ψ⟩ is the initial state, U is a unitary operator, and |ψ′⟩ is the final state. Note that this postulate explicitly describes closed systems, which do not interact with any external environment. Technically, there are no true closed systems in nature (unless you count the entire universe itself which may also not be closed), however, unitary operators can describe real-world quantum system dynamics.

An operator is unitary if:

\[ U^\dagger U = I \tag{1.21} \]

Where † (pronounced dagger) indicates the conjugate-transpose from earlier and I is the identity matrix:

\[ I = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.22} \]

So that if:

\[ U = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.23} \]

Then,

\[ U^\dagger = \begin{pmatrix} a^* & c^* \\ b^* & d^* \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.24} \]

Unitarity preserves norms in accordance with Postulate 1. For example, we cannot have an operator which acts on a quantum state such that its inner product with itself (after the operation) does not equal 1 for that violates Postulate 1.

In general, the inner product of ⟨ϕ| and |ψ⟩ is:

\[ \langle \phi | \psi \rangle \tag{1.25} \]

Pauli Matrices

The Pauli matrices are operators which act on the quantum state spaces. The three Pauli matrices are commonly known as the X-gate, Y-gate, and Z-gate and can be represented as:

\[ X = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad Y = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad Z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.26} \]

Confirm that each Pauli matrix is unitary by calculating U†U.

Next apply each matrix to the general qubit state:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle \tag{1.27} \]

The results are:

\[ X|\psi\rangle = \alpha|1\rangle + \beta|0\rangle \tag{1.28} \]

\[ Y|\psi\rangle = i\alpha|1\rangle - i\beta|0\rangle \tag{1.29} \]

\[ Z|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle - \beta|1\rangle \tag{1.30} \]

Comparing the results to the initial qubit state we can see that the X-gate causes a bit flip, the Y-gate yields a bit flip and a phase flip, and the Z-gate does a phase flip only.

In physics, visualizations are often helpful so we will provide a commonly used representation to show what a unitary operator such as a Pauli matrix does to a qubit.

Recall that a qubit can be represented by α and β where α and β are complex numbers satisfying |α|² + |β|² = 1.

First we write the complex amplitudes in polar form by drawing them in the complex plane. We can see that:

\[ \alpha = |\alpha| e^{i\phi_0} \tag{1.31} \]

Conventionally, α = r₀e^{iϕ₀}.

The same process can be done for β so that we have:

\[ \beta = |\beta| e^{i\phi_1} \tag{1.32} \]

Now we have a relationship between α, β and their phases ϕ₀ and ϕ₁.

To make the notation cleaner we use Euler’s identity:

\[ e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta \tag{1.33} \]

Now we can write our qubit state as:

\[ |\psi\rangle = |\alpha|e^{i\phi_0}|0\rangle + |\beta|e^{i\phi_1}|1\rangle \tag{1.34} \]

After factoring out a global phase we have:

\[ |\psi\rangle = e^{i\phi_0} \left( |\alpha||0\rangle + |\beta|e^{i(\phi_1-\phi_0)}|1\rangle \right) \tag{1.35} \]

Now we make a slight notation change to match conventions:

\[ |\alpha| = \cos\frac{\theta}{2}, \quad |\beta| = \sin\frac{\theta}{2}, \quad \phi = \phi_1 - \phi_0 \tag{1.36} \]

The Hadamard Gate

The Hadamard Gate is another commonly used unitary operator. It is expressed as:

\[ H = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.49} \]

When a Hadamard gate acts on a single bit such as |0⟩ the following is the result:

\[ H|0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle) \tag{1.50} \]

So, the Hadamard gate creates superposition!

Show that the superposition above is a valid quantum state. Hint: invoke Postulate 1 (normalization).

To do this, invoke Postulate 1. Meaning, does ⟨ψ|ψ⟩ = 1? Since |0⟩ and |1⟩ are orthonormal, then ⟨0|0⟩ = 1 and ⟨1|1⟩ = 1.

\[ \langle \psi | \psi \rangle = \frac{1}{2} \left( \langle 0|0\rangle + \langle 1|1\rangle \right) = 1 \tag{1.51} \]

You can verify that the Hadamard operator is unitary so that H†H = I, therefore:

\[ H^\dagger H = I \tag{1.52} \]

We say unitary operations preserve inner products.

Postulate 2′ of Quantum Mechanics

The time evolution of the state of a closed quantum system is described by the Schrodinger Equation:

\[ i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}|\psi(t)\rangle = H|\psi(t)\rangle \tag{1.53} \]

Where H is the Hamiltonian (not the Hadamard gate; be careful!). The Hamiltonian is an operator which generates time evolution. This can be “derived” from Postulate 2 by including explicit time-dependence.

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = U(t)|\psi(0)\rangle \tag{1.54} \]

Where |ψ(t)⟩ is the quantum state vector at some time t, |ψ(0)⟩ is the quantum state vector at some initial time t = 0, and U(t) is a time-evolution operator that tells us how the initial state changes. We require that:

1. The state changes smoothly with t.
2. Evolving first by t₁ and then by t₂ should be the same as evolving by t₁ + t₂:

\[ U(t_1)U(t_2) = U(t_1+t_2) \tag{1.55} \]

3. U(t) must be unitary to preserve the inner product.

The only continuous, unitary operators that satisfy the composition rule (requirement #2) have exponential form:

\[ U(t) = e^{-iAt} \tag{1.56} \]

Where A is a Hermitian operator. A Hermitian operator obeys the following:

\[ A^\dagger = A \tag{1.57} \]

Why should A be Hermitian? Because U(t) must be unitary:

\[ U^\dagger U = I \;\Rightarrow\; A^\dagger = A \tag{1.58} \]

Now we take the time derivative of U(t) to see what happens.

\[ \frac{dU(t)}{dt} = -iAU(t) \tag{1.59} \]

Which is the general form of the Schrodinger equation with the implication that A = H/ħ. Therefore, if we know H we know how the system evolves in time. The time-evolution unitary operator can then be written as:

\[ U(t) = e^{-iHt/\hbar} \tag{1.60} \]

So, we now have two equations which dictate how states evolve in quantum mechanics:

1. The Schrodinger Equation

\[ i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}|\psi(t)\rangle = H|\psi(t)\rangle \tag{1.61} \]

2. Updated Postulate 2

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = U(t)|\psi(0)\rangle \tag{1.62} \]

In the next exercise, use the updated Postulate 2 and the Schrodinger equation to show that the:

\[ H|\psi(0)\rangle \propto |\psi(0)\rangle \tag{1.63} \]

Show that:

\[ H|\psi(0)\rangle \propto |\psi(0)\rangle \tag{1.63} \]

The proportionality constant is commonly denoted E for reasons explained below. So, we have:

\[ H|\psi\rangle = E|\psi\rangle \tag{1.64} \]

Where we have dropped the function of time notation for simplicity. This gives a result known as the time-independent Schrodinger equation. If an operator acts on a state vector and the state vector is returned multiplied by a constant, the state is called an eigenstate of the operator, and the constant is its eigenvalue.

The Hermiticity of the Hamiltonian forces the eigenvalues to be real and therefore we can assign the measurable allowed energies of the system to the eigenvalues while the eigenstates are known more specifically as the energy eigenstates. Another way of writing this is:

\[ H|E\rangle = E|E\rangle \tag{1.65} \]

The |E⟩ are also known as stationary states of the system because they do not change with time.

Postulate 3 of Quantum Mechanics

Quantum measurements are described by a collection of measurement operators {Mm}. These act on the state space of the system being measured. The index m refers to the measurement outcomes that may occur. If the state of the system is |ψ⟩ immediately before the measurement then the probability that result m occurs is given by:

\[ p(m) = \langle \psi | M_m^\dagger M_m | \psi \rangle \tag{1.66} \]

And the state after the measurement is given by:

\[ |\psi'\rangle = \frac{M_m|\psi\rangle} {\sqrt{\langle \psi | M_m^\dagger M_m | \psi \rangle}} \tag{1.67} \]

The measurement operators must satisfy the Completeness Relation:

\[ \sum_m M_m^\dagger M_m = I \tag{1.68} \]

Which guarantees the probabilities must sum to one. Until now nothing about quantum mechanics is inherently strange (with the possible exception being the concept of the state vector). Postulate 3 introduces probabilistic outcomes to the theory, departing from classical determinism.

Let’s now explore the measurement operators Mm in the computational basis. When a measurement operator acts on a qubit it returns an outcome according to Postulate 3. Two obvious outcomes would be either |0⟩ or |1⟩. Therefore,

\[ M_0|\psi\rangle = |0\rangle\langle 0|\psi\rangle, \quad M_1|\psi\rangle = |1\rangle\langle 1|\psi\rangle \tag{1.69} \]

Note this implicitly restricts us to projective measurements. Now we solve the following systems representing the two outcome:

\[ M_0 \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ \beta \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.70} \]

The result is:

\[ M_0 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.71} \]

Now we express this as a projector, meaning:

\[ M_0 = |0\rangle\langle 0| \tag{1.72} \]

Therefore, a = 1, c = 1, b = 0, and d = 0 so that:

\[ M_0 = |0\rangle\langle 0| \tag{1.73} \]

The same process can be done for M1 giving:

\[ M_1 = |1\rangle\langle 1| \tag{1.74} \]

Now we check the probabilities from Postulate 3 using the computational measurement operators. The probability of obtaining outcome 0 is:

\[ p(0) = \langle \psi | M_0^\dagger M_0 | \psi \rangle \tag{1.75} \]

After carefully distributing:

\[ p(0) = |\alpha|^2 \tag{1.76} \]

Likewise,

\[ p(1) = |\beta|^2 \tag{1.77} \]

These are very important results! Postulate 3 dictates that the complex amplitudes are in fact probabilities! That is why earlier we referred to them as probability amplitudes. The squared modulus of the complex coefficients represents the measurement outcome probability.

The final states after measurement are:

\[ |\psi'\rangle = |0\rangle \quad \text{or} \quad |\psi'\rangle = |1\rangle \tag{1.78} \]

Given these measurement operators in the computational basis, show that the Completeness Relation is obeyed: Σₘ M†ₘMₘ = I.

Using the computational basis measurement operators M₀ = |0⟩⟨0| and M₁ = |1⟩⟨1|, verify explicitly that M₀†M₀ + M₁†M₁ = I.

An important theorem known as Spectral Decomposition states that any normal operator which acts on a vector space, V, is diagonal with respect to some orthonormal basis for V. A normal operator obeys MM† = M†M and thus Hermitian operators, M = M† are normal. The full derivation is shown in the appendix.

In general, the spectral decomposition of a Hermitian operator is given by:

\[ M = \sum_i \lambda_i |a_i\rangle\langle a_i| \tag{1.79} \]

Where λi are the eigenvalues of M and |ai⟩⟨ai| are the projectors onto the eigenspace of M.

The reference textbook by Nielsen and Chuang uses the following notation to represent the same expression:

\[ M = \sum_m m P_m \tag{1.80} \]

In general, our set of measurement operators {Mm} may not be projectors of this form, but when they are we call them projective measurement operators. Therefore, when Mm = Pm we have projective measurements.

A projector has the following properties:

\[ P^\dagger = P \tag{1.81} \]

\[ P^2 = P \tag{1.82} \]

That is projectors are Hermitian and idempotent. So, for projective measurements Postulate 3 becomes:

\[ p(m) = \langle \psi | P_m | \psi \rangle \tag{1.83} \]

Which represents the probability of getting results, m, and is commonly provided as one of the first postulates in undergraduate quantum mechanics. Additionally, the post measurement state is:

\[ |\psi'\rangle = \frac{P_m|\psi\rangle} {\sqrt{\langle \psi | P_m | \psi \rangle}} \tag{1.84} \]

An Application of Postulate 3: Distinguishing Quantum States

Classically, distinct states of an object are usually distinguishable, however, for quantum states the story is not so straightforward. As an example, we are given the following states and are asked to distinguish them in the computational basis by measuring.

\[ |\psi_1\rangle = |0\rangle, \quad |\psi_2\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle) \tag{1.85} \]

The measurement operators in the computational basis are M0 = |0⟩⟨0| and M1 = |1⟩⟨1|. To distinguish the given states, we measure to see if the outcomes are different. The probability of getting outcome 0 is given by Postulate 3:

\[ p_1(0) = 1 \tag{1.86} \]

\[ p_2(0) = \frac{1}{2} \tag{1.87} \]

These results indicate we cannot distinguish states which are not orthonormal to each other using quantum measurements. You can verify that ⟨ψ1|ψ2⟩ ≠ 0.

From probability theory, the average or expectation value of an outcome is given by:

\[ \langle M \rangle = \sum_m m\,p(m) \tag{1.88} \]

Which leads to the familiar expression of the standard deviation:

\[ (\Delta M)^2 = \langle M^2 \rangle - \langle M \rangle^2 \tag{1.89} \]

The spread of observed values upon measurement of M is represented by the standard deviation. This in combination with the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality give rise to the Generalized Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle (derived in the Appendix) which states for two observables C and D:

\[ \Delta C\,\Delta D \ge \frac{1}{2}\left| \langle [C,D] \rangle \right| \tag{1.90} \]

Where the commutator [C, D] = CD − DC.

Phase: Another Consequence of Postulate 3

We stated in previous sections that “we are free to multiply by a global phase factor because nothing physically changes upon doing so”. We will now prove this using Postulate 3. Consider the states |ψ⟩ and eiθ|ψ⟩. Now suppose Mm is a measurement operator associated with a quantum measurement as before. According to Postulate 3:

\[ p(m) = \langle \psi | M_m^\dagger M_m | \psi \rangle \tag{1.91} \]

And

\[ p(m) = \langle \psi | e^{-i\theta} M_m^\dagger M_m e^{i\theta} | \psi \rangle \tag{1.92} \]

Because e−iθeiθ = 1. So, from an observational standpoint these two states are identical. These are known as Global Phases and are irrelevant for the observed properties of the physical system.

Another type of phase is known as Relative Phase and has observable consequences on quantum states. Consider the two orthogonal states which differ by a relative phase:

\[ |\psi_+\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle), \quad |\psi_-\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle - |1\rangle) \tag{1.93} \]

We saw previously that orthogonal states have perfect distinguishability. In the next exercise, show that these states are orthogonal. This indicates that relative phase between produces distinct observable outcomes upon measurement.

Orthogonality guarantees that quantum states are distinguishable in principle, but whether that distinguishability appears in a given experiment depends on the measurement basis. Relative phase is physically meaningful because it changes measurement statistics for some observables, whereas global phase never does.

We can see this by attempting to use Postulate 3 in the computational basis:

\[ M = \sum_i \lambda_i |a_i\rangle\langle a_i| \tag{1.96} \]

This works for any complete orthonormal basis of eigenvectors (which we have!).

In our case:

\[ M = |\psi_+\rangle\langle \psi_+| - |\psi_-\rangle\langle \psi_-| \tag{1.97} \]

Postulate 4 of Quantum Mechanics

The state space of a composite physical system is the tensor product of the state space of the component physical systems. If we have systems numbered 1 through n and system i is prepared in the state |ψi⟩, then the joint state of the total system is:

\[ |\Psi\rangle = |\psi_1\rangle \otimes |\psi_2\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes |\psi_n\rangle \tag{1.99} \]

Where ⊗ represents the tensor product. A tensor product is a vector space equipped with a bilinear map.

To understand tensor products, we’ll start with a concrete example. Given that V and W are finite dimensional vector spaces where V belongs to ℝⁿ and W belongs to ℝᵐ for n and m as positive integers.

Therefore, the vector v has n values while vector w has m values. We now examine V ⊗ W. This tensor product is an mn dimensional vector space where the elements are linear combinations of v ⊗ w.

For example, if V is a 2-dimensional vector space with basis vectors |0⟩ and |1⟩ then |0⟩⊗|0⟩ + |1⟩⊗|1⟩ is an element of V ⊗ W.

The tensor product satisfies the following properties from linear algebra:

Property 1: z(v ⊗ w) = (zv) ⊗ w = v ⊗ (zw) for scalar z.

Property 2: (v1 + v2) ⊗ w = v1 ⊗ w + v2 ⊗ w.

Property 3: v ⊗ (w1 + w2) = v ⊗ w1 + v ⊗ w2.

Using explicit vectors, verify Properties 2 and 3 of the tensor product by expanding both sides of each equation and showing they are equal.

Now, if a system A is in |v⟩ and a system B is in |w⟩ then we may write the joint state as |v⟩|w⟩.

But it is not clear what this notation implies just yet.

First, notice that if |v⟩|w⟩ is a valid quantum mechanical state, then z|v⟩|w⟩ is also a valid state because we can always multiply state vectors by complex numbers.

Additionally, Hilbert spaces (where the state vectors live) are linear vector spaces in which composite states must respect scalar multiplication in each vector space independently.

\[ z(|v\rangle \otimes |w\rangle) = (z|v\rangle)\otimes |w\rangle \tag{1.100} \]

As well as:

\[ z(|v\rangle \otimes |w\rangle) = |v\rangle \otimes (z|w\rangle) \tag{1.101} \]

So, if we were to define |v⟩|w⟩ ≡ |v⟩ ⊗ |w⟩ it would readily obey Property 1 by default.

Now, let’s say that some states |v1⟩ and |v2⟩ are states of system A and are superposed such that:

\[ |v\rangle = |v_1\rangle + |v_2\rangle \tag{1.102} \]

This is a valid state although it is unnormalized. The composite system would be:

\[ |v\rangle|w\rangle = |v_1\rangle|w\rangle + |v_2\rangle|w\rangle \tag{1.103} \]

Which obeys Property 2 if |v⟩|w⟩ ≡ |v⟩ ⊗ |w⟩.

It is beginning to look more and more like the object |v⟩|w⟩ is indeed a tensor product.

Let’s finalize this result. We could of course say that system B has superposed states of |w1⟩ and |w2⟩ which are valid.

Thus, the composite is:

\[ |v\rangle(|w_1\rangle + |w_2\rangle) = |v\rangle|w_1\rangle + |v\rangle|w_2\rangle \tag{1.104} \]

Which is exactly Property 3 as long as |v⟩|w⟩ ≡ |v⟩ ⊗ |w⟩.

Since the object |v⟩|w⟩ obeys all the properties of a tensor product we say that it is a tensor product (up to isomorphism).

Operationally, the tensor product can be computed using the Kronecker Product for matrices.

\[ \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \end{pmatrix} \otimes \begin{pmatrix} c \\ d \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} ac \\ ad \\ bc \\ bd \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.105} \]

Compute the tensor product |0⟩⊗|1⟩ and |1⟩⊗|0⟩ using the Kronecker product of column vectors. Write each result as a 4-component column vector.

Lastly, here is common notation concerning tensor products:

\[ |\psi\rangle^{\otimes k} = \underbrace{|\psi\rangle \otimes \cdots \otimes |\psi\rangle}_{k\ \text{times}} \tag{1.106} \]

Additionally, physicists commonly use the shorthand |vw⟩ = |v⟩⊗|w⟩ for more compact expression.
