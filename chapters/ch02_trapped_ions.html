Trapped Ions

In this section we transition discuss the physical system that will ultimately serve as our quantum computer: trapped atomic ions. In 2000, D. P. DiVincenzo published, The physical implementation of quantum computation, in Fortschritte der Physik: Progress of Physics, 48, 771-783 which outlined five criteria for required for the realization of a quantum computer.

DiVicenzo's Criteria:

(1) (a) Qubits must be well characterized and easy to produce. (b) the system must be scalable, i.e. must be able to manage an arbitrary number of qubits.

(2) It must be possible to reliably initialize a qubit to a fiducial state, e.g. |0⟩;

(3) A qubit must be stable over timescales larger than the typical time needed to operate on it. The lifespan of a qubit over which it can maintain its quantum information content (before noise starts to destroy it) is known as coherence time. For a viable QC system, the coherence time of the qubit must be greater than the computation time.

(4) It must be possible to implement a universal set of quantum gates.

(5) A reliable procedure must exist to read out the state of a qubit.

While there are several candidates which can fulfill these criteria, we focus on atomic ions because in isolation, atoms naturally satisfy DiVincenzo's criteria via internal atomic states, which are identical from atom to atom,

How can a charged particle be confined in space at all?

2.1 Why Ions?

Ions are attractive candidates for quantum computation because they combine long-lived internal quantum states with excellent isolation from the environment. Unlike solid-state systems, individual ions can be prepared, controlled, and measured with extremely high fidelity using electromagnetic fields and laser light.

However, ions are charged particles, and charged particles respond strongly to electric fields. This makes confinement both possible and nontrivial. To appreciate why ion trapping is subtle, we begin with an important limitation of classical electromagnetism.

2.2 Earnshaw’s Theorem and the Failure of Static Traps

Consider an ion placed in a static electric potential. The force on the ion is determined by the gradient of the electric potential, while the stability of the equilibrium depends on the curvature of that potential.

Earnshaw’s theorem states that it is impossible to achieve stable confinement of a charged particle using only static electric fields in free space.

If static electric fields cannot trap ions, we must look for an alternative.

2.3 Time-Dependent Electric Fields

The key insight behind ion trapping is that Earnshaw’s theorem applies only to static fields. By allowing the electric potential to vary in time, it becomes possible to achieve effective confinement.

In radio-frequency (RF) ion traps, electric fields oscillate rapidly compared to the typical motion of the ion. Although the instantaneous force may not be confining, the time-averaged motion can be stable.

This idea underlies the operation of the Paul trap, which is the central trapping technology used in modern trapped-ion quantum computers.

2.9 Outlook: From Classical Motion to Quantum Mechanics

In this chapter we have treated the ion’s motion classically. However, once the ion is sufficiently cold, its motional degrees of freedom must be described quantum mechanically.

In the next chapter we will quantize the ion’s motion, introduce phonon modes, and connect this picture to the internal electronic states of the ion. These ingredients together form the foundation of trapped-ion quantum computation.
