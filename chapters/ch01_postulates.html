Postulates of Quantum Mechanics

Earlier we learned that computers need something that can switch between two or more different configurations. In a classical system, we identify these as bits which can be either “on” or “off”. From now on we will refer to “on” as 1 and “off” as 0. This is where the modern power switch symbols come from. Classical bits can only ever be 1 or 0 meaning their classical state is either 1 or 0. In a quantum system, the quantum states function as our bits and are therefore called quantum bits or qubits. As mentioned previously, qubits can be in a superposition of 1 and 0 before measurement which allows for useful pre-measurement manipulation before collapsing to 1 or 0. The idea of a quantum state or qubit is a logical departure from the concreteness of the working principle of a transistor. Let us focus on the meaning of a quantum state.

We begin with the Postulates of Quantum Mechanics in an attempt to set the framework for which quantum computing is performed. If and when you get confused about “why” its good practice to remember the definition of a postulate: a thing suggested or assumed as true as the basis for reasoning, discussion, or belief.

Postulate 2′ of Quantum Mechanics

The time evolution of the state of a closed quantum system is described by the Schrodinger Equation:

(1.53) [Insert Equation Here — iħ ∂|ψ(t)⟩/∂t = H|ψ(t)⟩]

Where H is the Hamiltonian (not the Hadamard gate; be careful!). The Hamiltonian is an operator which generates time evolution. This can be “derived” from Postulate 2 by including explicit time-dependence.

(1.54) [Insert Equation Here — |ψ(t)⟩ = U(t)|ψ(0)⟩]

Where |ψ(t)⟩ is the quantum state vector at some time t, |ψ(0)⟩ is the quantum state vector at some initial time t = 0, and U(t) is a time-evolution operator that tells us how the initial state changes. We require that:

1. The state changes smoothly with t.
2. Evolving first by t₁ and then by t₂ should be the same as evolving by t₁ + t₂:

(1.55) [Insert Equation Here — U(t₁)U(t₂) = U(t₁+t₂)]

3. U(t) must be unitary to preserve the inner product.

The only continuous, unitary operators that satisfy the composition rule (requirement #2) have exponential form:

(1.56) [Insert Equation Here — U(t) = e^{-iAt} ]

Where A is a Hermitian operator. A Hermitian operator obeys the following:

(1.57) [Insert Equation Here — A† = A]

Why should A be Hermitian? Because U(t) must be unitary:

(1.58) [Insert Equation Here — U†U = I → A† = A]

Now we take the time derivative of U(t) to see what happens.

If an operator acts on a state vector and the state vector is returned multiplied by a constant, the state is called an eigenstate of the operator, and the constant is its eigenvalue.

The Hermiticity of the Hamiltonian forces the eigenvalues to be real and therefore we can assign the measurable allowed energies of the system to the eigenvalues while the eigenstates are known more specifically as the energy eigenstates. Another way of writing this is:

(1.65) [Insert Equation Here — H|E⟩ = E|E⟩]

The |E⟩ are also known as stationary states of the system because they do not change with time.
