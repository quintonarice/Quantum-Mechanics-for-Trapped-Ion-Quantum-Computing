All the Physics You Need for Ion-Trapped Quantum Computing

A Beginner-Friendly Guide to Ion-Trap Quantum Computing

By Quinton Rice

Motivation

You may have heard of quantum computing, or you may be interested in the future of quantum information science. You might also be familiar with classical computers since the majority of us have one either in our pocket (cellphone) or somewhere close by (laptop/desktop). Have you ever stopped to wonder how these devices work on a fundamental level? How can a combination of circuits and microchips be combined to give us the most technologically advanced device in human history? This book will not answer that question. Instead, we note that since it is possible to create such a device using classical techniques, it should also be possible to use quantum techniques. The quantum approach brings both advantages and disadvantages.

You may recall images of the very first classical computers—giant refrigerator-shaped machines that filled entire rooms and required teams of scientists to operate. These computers were so large because they used vacuum tube technology as the building block for computation. A vacuum tube is exactly what it sounds like: an enclosed glass tube in which all the air has been removed. Inside there are three components:

  1. Cathode: a heated wire that emits electrons through thermionic emission.
  2. Anode: a positively charged metal surface that attracts the electrons.
  3. Control grid: a fine metal mesh placed between the cathode and anode.

The cathode is connected to the negative terminal of a power supply, while the anode is connected to the positive terminal. The control grid can be neutral or negatively charged; if neutral, electrons flow freely to the anode (“on”), and if negative, electrons are repelled (“off”). This forms the basic binary switch needed for digital computation.

Some early digital computers used around 17,000 vacuum tubes, each a few inches in size. These were essentially tiny light bulbs, consuming significant power and generating heat.

In 1947, John Bardeen, Walter Brattain, and William Shockley built the first transistor. The transistor plays the same logical role as the vacuum tube, only now it is a solid-state semiconductor device. The simplest type is the bipolar junction transistor (BJT), operationally identical to the vacuum tube. Its components are:

  1. Emitter: source of electrons.
  2. Collector: receives electrons.
  3. Base: controls electron flow based on applied voltage.

In an NPN transistor, the emitter and collector are n-type semiconductors (excess electrons), while the base is p-type (excess holes). Doping and voltage control determine whether current flows (“on”) or not (“off”). This is the basis of classical bits.

As transistor sizes shrank to nanometer scales—approaching fewer than one hundred atoms in length—quantum effects such as tunneling and interference became unavoidable. Instead of fighting these effects, physicists embraced them as the foundation for a fundamentally new type of computer: the quantum computer.

A quantum computer switches between quantum states rather than classical states. Quantum states can exist in superpositions, meaning they can be both “on” and “off” simultaneously before measurement. Consider a light switch analogy: classically it is either “on” or “off,” but a quantum light switch can be in between until it is measured. The power of quantum computing lies in manipulating states before measurement.
