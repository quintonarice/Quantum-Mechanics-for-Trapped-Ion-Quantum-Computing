Quantized Motion of Trapped Ions

In the previous chapter, we described the motion of a trapped ion using classical mechanics. By introducing the pseudopotential approximation, we showed that the ion’s secular motion is well described by a harmonic potential with an experimentally tunable frequency.

However, this description implicitly assumes that the ion’s position and momentum can take on arbitrary continuous values. When the thermal energy greatly exceeds the motional energy spacing $\hbar \omega_{sec}$, the discrete level structure is experimentally unresolved and the motion is well approximated as continuous. Once the ion is cooled such that $k_B T \leq \hbar \omega_{sec}$, this continuum approximation fails and the quantized nature of the motional degrees of freedom becomes essential. At that point, the ion’s motional degrees of freedom must be treated quantum mechanically.

In this chapter, we apply the postulates of quantum mechanics to the motional degrees of freedom of a trapped ion. We will see that the classical harmonic motion derived previously naturally becomes a quantum harmonic oscillator, with discrete energy levels and quantized excitations known as phonons.

Throughout this chapter, we focus exclusively on the ion’s secular motion described by the pseudopotential. The fast micromotion, which is phase-locked to the RF drive, will be treated as a classical modulation and will not be quantized.

Classical to Quantum

We start by recalling that the classical Hamilitonian describes the total energy of a physical system and is expressed by the sum of the kinetic and potential energies:

\[ \ H = T + V \tag{3.1} \]

where the kinectic energy is commonly expressed in terms of momentum $T = \frac{P^2}{2m}$. The potential energy is the total potential energy we derived previously:

\[ \ V = U_{tot} = \frac{1}{2} m \omega_{sec}'^2 (X^2 + Y^2) + \frac{1}{2} m \omega_z^2 Z^2 \tag{3.2} \]

For now, we will restrict our quantization to the axial motion returning later the the radial motion as we will see the process is identical. Therefore:

\[ \ H = \frac{P_Z^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2} m \omega_z^2 Z^2 \tag{3.3} \]

The classical secular axial motion of a trapped ion is therefore governed by the Hamiltonian of a one-dimensional harmonic oscillator. According to Postulate $2'$, the time evolution of a quantum system is governed by the Schrödinger equation. Applying this postulate to the ion’s axial motion, requires that we promote the classical representation of position and momentum their quantum representation such that $Z \rightarrow \hat Z$ and $P_Z \rightarrow -i \hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial Z}$ then the Hamilitonian operator is given by:

\[ \ \hat H = \frac{\hat P_Z^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2} m \omega_z^2 \hat Z^2 \tag{3.4} \]

The Schrodinger Equation then tells us that:

\[ \ i \hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} |\psi (Z,t) \rangle = \hat H |\psi (Z,t) \rangle \tag{3.5} \]

Explicitly, we have:

\[ \ i \hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t} |\psi (Z,t) \rangle = [-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial Z^2} + \frac{1}{2} m \omega_z^2 Z^2]|\psi (Z,t) \rangle \tag{3.6} \]

Because the Hamiltonian is time-independent, we seek solutions of separable form:

\[ \ |\psi (Z,t) \rangle = \phi(Z) e^{\frac{-iEt}{\hbar}} \tag{3.7} \]

Upon substituting equation 3.7 into equation 3.6 we arrive at:

\[ \ [-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{\partial^2}{\partial Z^2} + \frac{1}{2} m \omega_z^2 Z^2]|\phi(Z) = E \phi (Z) \tag{3.8} \]

You may remember from introductory quantum mechanics that the harmonic oscillator can be solved algebraically using ladder operators. This approach not only simplifies the calculation of energy eigenvalues, but also reveals a deep physical structure underlying the motion. We now apply this method to the axial motion of a trapped ion.

Our goal is to express the Hamiltonian in terms of operators that raise or lower the energy of a given motional state by a fixed amount. Then, the so-called ladder operators are defined as:

\[ \begin{align} \hat a &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \hbar m \omega_z}} \left( m \omega_z \hat Z + i \hat P_Z \right) \\[6pt] \hat a^\dagger &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2 \hbar m \omega_z}} \left( m \omega_z \hat Z - i \hat P_Z \right) \end{align} \tag{3.9} \]

The ladder operators are dimensionless, linear combinations of position and momentum constructed by P. A. M. Dirac to simplify the Hamilitonian and are commonly known as the creation, $\hat a^\dagger$, and annihilation operators, $\hat a$. Some texts use the terminology raising and lowering operators as well. It should be noted and verified by the reader that $[\hat a, \hat a^\dagger] = 1$ which follows from the canonical commutation relation $[\hat Z, \hat P_Z] = i \hbar$.

The exercise 3.1 below requires verification that the Hamiltonian can be re-written in terms of the ladder operators:

\[ \ \hat H = \frac{\hat P_Z^2}{2m} + \frac{1}{2} m \omega_z^2 \hat Z^2 = \hbar \omega_z \big (\hat a^\dagger \hat a + \frac{1}{2}) \tag{3.10} \]

Perhaps the easiest way is to simply plug in the ladder operators and brute force the algebra keeping the commutation relation in mind.

Verify explicitly equation 3.10.

We now demonstrate why the ladder operators are called creation/raising and annihilation/lowering operators by considering the action of the lowering operator on an energy eigenstate $|E\rangle$ where we assume $| E \rangle$ is normalized. The Hamiltonian is linear so we may write:

\[ \ \hat H \big (\hat a | E \rangle) = \big (\hat H \hat a ) | E \rangle \tag{3.11} \]

Using the commutation of $\hat H$ and $\hat a$ we have:

\[ \begin{align} \ [\hat H, \hat a] & = \hat H \hat a - \hat a \hat H \\ & = \hbar \omega_z \big (\hat a^\dagger \hat a + \frac{1}{2}) \hat a - \hat a \hbar \omega_z \big (\hat a^\dagger \hat a + \frac{1}{2}) \\ & = \hbar \omega_z \big (\hat a^\dagger \hat a \hat a - \hat a \hat a^\dagger \hat a) \\ & = -\hbar \omega_z \hat a \end{align} \tag{3.12} \]

where we have used $[\hat a, \hat a^\dagger] = 1$ to express $\hat a \hat a^\dagger = \hat a^\dagger \hat a + 1$. Now we have:

\[ \ [\hat H, \hat a] = \hat H \hat a - \hat a \hat H \rightarrow \hat H \hat a = \hat a \hat H - \hbar \omega_z \hat a \tag{3.13} \]

Now plugging $\hat a \hat H$ into the right side of equation 3.11:

\[ \begin{align} \ \hat H \big (\hat a | E \rangle) & = \big (\hat a \hat H - \hbar \omega_z \hat a ) | E \rangle \\ & = \hat a \hat H | E \rangle - \hbar \omega_z \hat a | E \rangle \\ \end{align} \tag{3.14} \]

Noticing that $\hat H | E \rangle = E | E \rangle$ from equation 1.65 we have:

\[ \begin{align} \ \hat H \big (\hat a | E \rangle) & = \hat a E | E \rangle - \hbar \omega_z \hat a | E \rangle \\ & = \big ( E - \hbar \omega_z ) \big (\hat a | E \rangle \\ \end{align} \tag{3.15} \]

So upon acting $\hat H$ upon $\hat a | E \rangle$ the same ket is returned multiplied by the factor $\big (E - \hbar \omega_z)$, which indicates that $\hat a | E \rangle$ is also an eigenstate of $\hat H$ with a lower energy eigenvalue than the original ket $| E \rangle$.

So we say that $\hat a$ has lowered the energy by $\hbar \omega_z$ and changed the corresponding eigenstate. Note that this new eigenstate is not normalized so we write $\hat a | E \rangle \propto | E - \hbar \omega_z \rangle$.

Show that the action of $\hat a^\dagger$ on an eigenstate of $\hat H$ produces an eigenvalue raised by $\hbar \omega_z$.

In quantum mechanics the total energy cannot of a particle cannot be below the potential energy minimum which ultimately means that the lowest energy state must not go below $E = 0$. This puts restrictions on the lowering operator as we shall see. We require then that:

\[ \ \hat a | E_0 \rangle = 0 \tag{3.16} \]

where $|E_0\rangle$ is the lowest possible energy state. This expression is often known as the ladder termination condition, and can be used to find the energy of the lowest state. Applying the Hamiltonian:

\[ \begin{align} \ \hat H |E_0 \rangle & = \hbar \omega_z \big (\hat a^\dagger \hat a + \frac{1}{2}) | E_0 \rangle \\ & = \hbar \omega_z \hat a^\dagger \hat a | E_0 \rangle + \frac{1}{2} \hbar \omega_z | E_0 \rangle \\ & = \frac{1}{2} \hbar \omega_z | E_0 \rangle \end{align} \tag{3.17} \]

Where in the second line we used equation 3.17, $\hat a | E_0 \rangle = 0$, to cancel the first term. This well known result shows that the lowest possible energy is not zero! Rather, $E_0 = \frac{1}{2} \hbar \omega_z$, known as the zero-point energy of the quantum harmonic oscillator.

We can perform a similar process as we did with equation 3.11 in which now we apply the Hamiltonian and the raising operator to the lowest energy eigenstate $|E_0\rangle$ keeping in mind the commutation of $[\hat H, \hat a^\dagger\]$ which means that $\hat H \hat a^\dagger = \hat a ^\dagger \hat H + \hbar \omega_z \hat a^\dagger$. Therefore:

\[ \ \hat H \big (\hat a^\dagger | E_0 \rangle ) = \big (\hat a^\dagger \hat H + \hbar \omega_z \hat a^\dagger) | E_0 \rangle \tag{3.18} \]

Again, we use the fact that $\hat H |E_0\rangle = E_0 |E_0 \rangle$ to write:

\[ \begin{align} \ \hat H \big (\hat a^\dagger | E_0 \rangle ) & = \hat a^\dagger E_0 | E_0 \rangle + \hbar \omega_z \hat a^\dagger |E_0\rangle \\ & = \big (E_0 + \hbar \omega_z) \hat a^\dagger | E_0 \rangle \end{align} \tag{3.19} \]

Using the previous result of equation 3.17 we have:

\[ \ \hat H \big (\hat a^\dagger | E_0 \rangle ) = \frac{3}{2} \hbar \omega_z \hat a^\dagger | E_0 \rangle \tag{3.20} \]

This process can be continously applied to produce fractional integers of $\hbar \omega_z$ so that energy spectrum may be written as:

\[ \ E_n = \hbar \omega_z \big (n + \frac{1}{2}) \tag{3.21} \]

where $n = 0, 1, 2, 3, ...$

As is common practice we use the quantum number $n$ to label the the energy eigenstates so that $|E_n\rangle = | n \rangle$.

Following the standard approach, we define the number operator:

\[ \ \hat N \equiv \hat a^\dagger a \tag{3.11} \]

If we now compute the commutators $[\hat N, \hat a]$ and $[\hat N, \hat a^\dagger]$, we see that:

\[ \begin{align} \ [\hat N, \hat a] &= -\hat a \\ [\hat N, \hat a^\dagger] &= +\hat a^\dagger \\ \end{align} \tag{3.12} \]

This means that $\hat a$ lowers eigenvalues of $\hat N$ while $\hat a^\dagger$ raises eigenvalues of $\hat N$.

(a) Show that if one eigenstate, $|\lambda\rangle$, exists: $\hat N|\lambda\rangle = \lambda |\lambda\rangle$ then there must be an infinte ladder.

(b) Compute the expectation value of $\hat N$ and show that the eigenvalues $\lambda \rightarrow 0,1,2...$

Based on Exercise 3.2, we now have an eigenvalue equation where we have switched to the common notation $\lambda \rightarrow n$:

\[ \ \hat N |n\rangle = n|n\rangle \tag{3.13} \]

Therefore:

\[ \ \hat H |n\rangle = \hbar \omega_z \big(\hat a^\dagger \hat a +\frac{1}{2}) |n\rangle = \hbar \omega_z \big(n + \frac{1}{2}) |n\rangle \tag{3.14} \]

So that the energy eigenvalues:

\[ \ E_n = \hbar \omega_z \big(n + \frac{1}{2}) \tag{3.15} \]

A well known result which is absolutely worth deriving. This result means that the axial motion is quantized, where each quantum of energy $\hbar \omega_z$ corresponds to one excitation. Amazingly, even when $n = 0$ the ion has zero-point motion with energy $E_0 = \frac{1}{2} \hbar \omega_z$.

The states $|n\rangle$ represent quantized motional energy levels of the trapped ion and are known as motional number state. In the next section, we interpret these discrete motional energy excitations as quanta of vibrational motion and introduce the concept of phonons.

Zero-Point Motion and Quantized Motional Excitations

We begin by solving the ladder operators in equation 3.9 for the position and momentum operators $\hat Z$ and $\hat P_Z$:

\[ \begin{align} \ \hat Z = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 m \omega_z}} \big (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger)\\ \hat P_Z = i \sqrt{\frac{\hbar m \omega_z}{2}} \big (\hat a^\dagger - \hat a) \\ \end{align} \tag{3.16} \]

These relations make explicit that position and momentum are built from creation and annihilation operators. We now consider the expectation value of the position of the ion using equation 1.88 from the Postulate 3 section. The expectation value of the position $Z$ is:

\[ \ \langle Z \rangle_{n} = \langle n | \hat Z | n \rangle = \sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2 m \omega_z}} \langle n | \big (\hat a + \hat a^\dagger) | n \rangle \tag{3.17} \]
