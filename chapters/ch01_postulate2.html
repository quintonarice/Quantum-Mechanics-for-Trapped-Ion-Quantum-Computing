Postulate 2 of Quantum Mechanics

The evolution of a closed quantum system is described by a unitary transformation.

\[ |\psi'\rangle = U|\psi\rangle \tag{1.20} \]

Where $|\psi\rangle$ is the initial state, $U$ is a unitary operator, and $\psi'\rangle$ is the final state. Note that this postulate explicitly describes closed systems, which do not interact with any external environment. Technically, there are no true closed systems in nature (unless you count the entire universe itself which may also not be closed), however, unitary operators can describe real-world quantum system dynamics.

An operator is unitary if:

\[ U^\dagger U = I \tag{1.21} \]

Where $\dagger$ (pronounced dagger) indicates the conjugate-transpose from earlier and I is the identity matrix:

\[ I = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.22} \]

So that if:

\[ U = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.23} \]

Then,

\[ U^\dagger = \begin{pmatrix} a^* & c^* \\ b^* & d^* \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.24} \]

Unitarity preserves norms in accordance with Postulate 1. For example, we cannot have an operator which acts on a quantum state such that its inner product with itself (after the operation) does not equal 1 for that violates Postulate 1.

In general, the inner product of $U|v\rangle$ and $U|w\rangle$ is:

\[ \langle v |U^\dagger U | w \rangle = \langle v |I| w\rangle = \langle v|w \rangle \tag{1.25} \]

There are some widely used unitary operators which are applicable to quantum computing and require our attention.

Pauli Matrices

The Pauli matrices are operators which act on the quantum state spaces. The three Pauli matrices are commonly known as the X-gate, Y-gate, and Z-gate and can be represented as:

\[ X = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad Y = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad Z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.26} \]

Confirm that each Pauli matrix is unitary by calculating U†U.

Next apply each matrix to the general qubit state:

\[ |\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle + \beta|1\rangle \tag{1.27} \]

The results are:

\[ X|\psi\rangle = \alpha|1\rangle + \beta|0\rangle \tag{1.28} \]

\[ Y|\psi\rangle = \alpha|1\rangle - \beta|0\rangle \tag{1.29} \]

\[ Z|\psi\rangle = \alpha|0\rangle - \beta|1\rangle \tag{1.30} \]

Where we have dropped the a factor of $i$ from the Y-gate which is explained later in the section on global phase.

Comparing the results to the initial qubit state we can see that the X-gate causes a bit flip, the Y-gate yields a bit flip and a phase flip, and the Z-gate does a phase flip only. There are many gates or unitary operators that exist, many of which we explore when discussing basic quantum computation.

In physics, visualizations are often helpful so we will provide a commonly used representation to show what a unitary operator such as a Pauli matrix does to a qubit.

To do this, first we write the complex amplitudes in polar form by drawing them in the complex plane. We can see that:

\[ \alpha = |\alpha| e^{i\phi_0} \tag{1.31} \]

Conventionally, α = r₀e^{iϕ₀}.

The same process can be done for β so that we have:

\[ \beta = |\beta| e^{i\phi_1} \tag{1.32} \]

Now we have a relationship between α, β and their phases ϕ₀ and ϕ₁.

To make the notation cleaner we use Euler’s identity:

\[ e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta \tag{1.33} \]

Now we can write our qubit state as:

\[ |\psi\rangle = |\alpha|e^{i\phi_0}|0\rangle + |\beta|e^{i\phi_1}|1\rangle \tag{1.34} \]

After factoring out a global phase we have:

\[ |\psi\rangle = e^{i\phi_0} \left( |\alpha||0\rangle + |\beta|e^{i(\phi_1-\phi_0)}|1\rangle \right) \tag{1.35} \]

Now we make a slight notation change to match conventions:

\[ |\alpha| = \cos\frac{\theta}{2}, \quad |\beta| = \sin\frac{\theta}{2}, \quad \phi = \phi_1 - \phi_0 \tag{1.36} \]

The Hadamard Gate

The Hadamard Gate is another commonly used unitary operator. It is expressed as:

\[ H = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \tag{1.49} \]

When a Hadamard gate acts on a single bit such as |0⟩ the following is the result:

\[ H|0\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |1\rangle) \tag{1.50} \]

So, the Hadamard gate creates superposition!

Show that the superposition above is a valid quantum state. Hint: invoke Postulate 1 (normalization).

To do this, invoke Postulate 1. Meaning, does ⟨ψ|ψ⟩ = 1? Since |0⟩ and |1⟩ are orthonormal, then ⟨0|0⟩ = 1 and ⟨1|1⟩ = 1.

\[ \langle \psi | \psi \rangle = \frac{1}{2} \left( \langle 0|0\rangle + \langle 1|1\rangle \right) = 1 \tag{1.51} \]

You can verify that the Hadamard operator is unitary so that H†H = I, therefore:

\[ H^\dagger H = I \tag{1.52} \]

We say unitary operations preserve inner products.

Postulate 2′ of Quantum Mechanics

The time evolution of the state of a closed quantum system is described by the Schrodinger Equation:

\[ i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}|\psi(t)\rangle = H|\psi(t)\rangle \tag{1.53} \]

Where H is the Hamiltonian (not the Hadamard gate; be careful!). The Hamiltonian is an operator which generates time evolution. This can be “derived” from Postulate 2 by including explicit time-dependence.

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = U(t)|\psi(0)\rangle \tag{1.54} \]

Where |ψ(t)⟩ is the quantum state vector at some time t, |ψ(0)⟩ is the quantum state vector at some initial time t = 0, and U(t) is a time-evolution operator that tells us how the initial state changes. We require that:

1. The state changes smoothly with t.
2. Evolving first by t₁ and then by t₂ should be the same as evolving by t₁ + t₂:

\[ U(t_1)U(t_2) = U(t_1+t_2) \tag{1.55} \]

3. U(t) must be unitary to preserve the inner product.

The only continuous, unitary operators that satisfy the composition rule (requirement #2) have exponential form:

\[ U(t) = e^{-iAt} \tag{1.56} \]

Where A is a Hermitian operator. A Hermitian operator obeys the following:

\[ A^\dagger = A \tag{1.57} \]

Why should A be Hermitian? Because U(t) must be unitary:

\[ U^\dagger U = I \;\Rightarrow\; A^\dagger = A \tag{1.58} \]

Now we take the time derivative of U(t) to see what happens.

\[ \frac{dU(t)}{dt} = -iAU(t) \tag{1.59} \]

Which is the general form of the Schrodinger equation with the implication that A = H/ħ. Therefore, if we know H we know how the system evolves in time. The time-evolution unitary operator can then be written as:

\[ U(t) = e^{-iHt/\hbar} \tag{1.60} \]

So, we now have two equations which dictate how states evolve in quantum mechanics:

1. The Schrodinger Equation

\[ i\hbar \frac{\partial}{\partial t}|\psi(t)\rangle = H|\psi(t)\rangle \tag{1.61} \]

2. Updated Postulate 2

\[ |\psi(t)\rangle = U(t)|\psi(0)\rangle \tag{1.62} \]

\[ H|\psi(0)\rangle \propto |\psi(0)\rangle \tag{1.63} \]

Show that:

\[ H|\psi(0)\rangle \propto |\psi(0)\rangle \tag{1.63} \]

The proportionality constant is commonly denoted E for reasons explained below. So, we have:

\[ H|\psi\rangle = E|\psi\rangle \tag{1.64} \]

Where we have dropped the function of time notation for simplicity. This gives a result known as the time-independent Schrodinger equation. If an operator acts on a state vector and the state vector is returned multiplied by a constant, the state is called an eigenstate of the operator, and the constant is its eigenvalue.

The Hermiticity of the Hamiltonian forces the eigenvalues to be real and therefore we can assign the measurable allowed energies of the system to the eigenvalues while the eigenstates are known more specifically as the energy eigenstates. Another way of writing this is:

\[ H|E\rangle = E|E\rangle \tag{1.65} \]

The |E⟩ are also known as stationary states of the system because they do not change with time.
